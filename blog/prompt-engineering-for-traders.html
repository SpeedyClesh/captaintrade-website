The Prompt Engineering Pattern That Makes Claude 3x More Useful for Traders

Most people treat AI like a chatbot. I treat it like a junior analyst. Here's the exact system prompt architecture I use to generate structured trade ideas from raw chart data.

CaptainTrade
Developer · AI Engineer · Trader
March 15, 2026 Published
5 min read Read time

The problem with how traders use AI

The typical trader's interaction with Claude or ChatGPT goes something like this: they paste in a chart description and ask "what do you think about this trade?" They get a long, balanced, hedge-everything response that says the trade could go up or it could go down, and they walk away no more informed than before.

The problem isn't the AI — it's the prompt. A vague question gets a vague answer. A precisely structured question, given to an AI that has been told exactly what role it is playing and what format to respond in, gets a dramatically different result.

Core principle

The quality of AI output is almost entirely a function of input quality. Garbage in, garbage out applies to language models more strictly than almost anything else in technology.

Building a proper system prompt

A system prompt is the instruction set you give the model before the conversation begins. It defines the role, the constraints, and the expected output format. Here's the base system prompt I use for trade analysis:

You are a professional technical analyst specialising in Forex and
Crypto markets. You have deep expertise in market structure, Smart
Money Concepts, and risk management.

When analysing a trade setup, you will:
1. Identify the higher timeframe bias (bullish/bearish/ranging)
2. Assess the quality of the entry trigger
3. Define precise invalidation levels
4. Rate the setup from 1-10 with clear reasoning
5. Respond ONLY in the JSON format specified — no prose, no caveats

If the setup is unclear or insufficient information is provided,
return a JSON object with "quality": 0 and a "reason" field.

Forcing structured output

The single most powerful thing you can do in a prompt is specify the exact output format. Instead of getting a paragraph of text you have to interpret, you get machine-readable JSON that you can log, compare, and act on directly.

Respond in this exact JSON format:
{
  "pair": "string",
  "htf_bias": "bullish | bearish | ranging",
  "entry_trigger": "string — describe the trigger in one sentence",
  "entry_zone": "number — price level",
  "stop_loss": "number — price level",
  "take_profit_1": "number",
  "take_profit_2": "number",
  "risk_reward": "number",
  "setup_quality": "number 1-10",
  "key_risks": ["string", "string"],
  "reasoning": "string — max 3 sentences"
}

Adding chain-of-thought reasoning

For more complex setups, I add a chain-of-thought instruction that forces the model to reason through the analysis before committing to an output. This significantly reduces hallucination and improves the quality of the reasoning field.

The instruction is simple: add "Think step by step before responding. Show your reasoning in a 'thinking' field before the final output." The model then works through the analysis systematically rather than pattern-matching to a generic response.

Full example prompt

Here's a complete user message that follows this architecture, combining chart description with structured request:

Analyse this setup:
Pair: EUR/USD
Daily: Bearish structure. Price broke below 1.0950 support last week.
H4: Retracing into a supply zone at 1.0920-1.0940.
H1: Price formed a bearish engulfing candle at the top of the supply zone.
Current price: 1.0925

Apply your analysis framework and return the structured JSON output.

Limitations to understand

AI cannot see charts — it works entirely from text descriptions. The quality of your description directly caps the quality of the analysis. Always include: the pair, the timeframe context, key levels price has respected, and the current price relative to those levels.

Treat the output as a second opinion from a junior analyst, not a signal to trade blindly. Use it to pressure-test your own analysis, identify risks you may have overlooked, and check your risk-to-reward calculation.
